Reading Comprehension Strategies

Tips and strategies to help you succeed on the reading comprehension portion of the Electrical aptitude test.

Overview

The reading comprehension section tests your ability to:

  • Understand and analyze written passages
  • Identify main ideas and supporting details
  • Make inferences and draw conclusions
  • Understand vocabulary in context
  • Recognize author's purpose and tone
General Reading Tips
  • Read the passage. I sort of skim a little bit, making note of where things are so I can come back and answer specific questions. It's unlikely I will remember the answer from reading it once; I'm reading it so I know where to find the information quickly.
  • Identify the main idea and key points as you read. Loosely figuring out the topic of each paragraph makes it easy to come back and answer specific questions.
  • Pay attention to topic sentences and concluding statements
  • Don't rely on prior knowledge - base answers only on the passage
Question Types

You'll encounter several types of questions:

  • Main Idea: Identify the central theme or primary purpose
  • Detail Questions: Find specific information stated in the passage
  • Inference Questions: Draw conclusions based on implied information
  • Vocabulary: Determine word meaning from context
  • Author's Purpose: Understand why the author wrote the passage
Example Passage and Questions
Reading Passage: The French Revolution and the Reign of Terror

The French Revolution, which began in 1789, marked one of the most tumultuous periods in European history. Sparked by widespread discontent with the absolute monarchy of Louis XVI, economic hardship, and the influence of Enlightenment ideas, the revolution would fundamentally reshape French society and politics. The initial phase saw the storming of the Bastille prison on July 14, 1789, an event that has since become France's national holiday. This symbolic act represented the people's rejection of royal authority and their determination to claim power for themselves.

As the revolution progressed, the National Assembly worked to dismantle the old feudal system and establish a constitutional monarchy. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, adopted in August 1789, proclaimed that all men were born free and equal in rights. However, these lofty ideals would soon be tested as the revolution took increasingly radical turns. The execution of Louis XVI in January 1793 marked a point of no return, transforming France from a constitutional monarchy into a republic.

The period known as the Reign of Terror, which lasted from September 1793 to July 1794, represents one of the darkest chapters of the revolution. Led by Maximilien Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety, this era was characterized by extreme measures designed to eliminate enemies of the revolution. The guillotine became the primary instrument of execution, and its efficiency made it a symbol of revolutionary justice—or injustice, depending on one's perspective.

The guillotine itself was a relatively new invention, having been developed by Dr. Joseph-Ignace Guillotin in 1792. Ironically, Dr. Guillotin had proposed the device as a more humane method of execution, believing it would provide a quick and painless death compared to the brutal methods used previously, such as hanging, beheading by sword, or drawing and quartering. The machine consisted of a heavy blade suspended between two upright posts, which would drop rapidly to sever the head from the body in a single motion.

During the Reign of Terror, the guillotine was used extensively in public executions that drew large crowds. Place de la Révolution (now Place de la Concorde) in Paris became the primary execution site, where thousands met their end. The most famous victim was undoubtedly Marie Antoinette, the former queen, who was executed on October 16, 1793. Her death, along with that of her husband Louis XVI, symbolized the complete destruction of the old regime.

The efficiency of the guillotine was both its strength and its horror. Unlike previous methods of execution that could be botched or prolonged, the guillotine delivered death almost instantaneously. Contemporary accounts describe the blade dropping in less than a second, with the head separated from the body before most observers could process what had happened. Some witnesses claimed that the severed heads showed signs of consciousness for several seconds after decapitation, though modern medical science suggests this was likely a misinterpretation of reflexive muscle movements.

Estimates suggest that approximately 16,000 to 17,000 people were executed by guillotine during the Reign of Terror, though the total number of deaths from the revolution's various forms of violence was much higher. The victims included not only aristocrats and royalty but also common people accused of counter-revolutionary activities. The definition of "enemy of the revolution" became increasingly broad, leading to a climate of fear and suspicion throughout France.

The Reign of Terror came to an end with the execution of Robespierre himself on July 28, 1794, in what became known as the Thermidorian Reaction. Ironically, the man who had orchestrated so many executions met the same fate he had prescribed for others. His death marked the beginning of a more moderate phase of the revolution, though the guillotine would continue to be used in France for executions until 1977, when capital punishment was finally abolished.

The French Revolution's legacy is complex and multifaceted. While it advanced principles of liberty, equality, and fraternity that would influence democratic movements worldwide, it also demonstrated how revolutionary ideals could be corrupted by extremism and violence. The guillotine, intended as a symbol of egalitarian justice, became instead a symbol of state-sanctified murder, reminding us that the tools of justice can easily become instruments of terror when placed in the wrong hands.

Main Idea Questions

These questions are about the overarching idea and theme of the passage. You should be able to answer these types of questions with an initial reading. Rule out answers that seem off.

1. What is the main idea of this passage?

Answer: B - The passage presents a balanced view of the revolution, highlighting both its positive contributions (democratic ideals) and its dark side (the Reign of Terror and guillotine executions).

A can be ruled out because the revolution was not peaceful. C is ruled out because it's too specific- the passage isn't only about the guillotine. D is ruled out because it is also too specific.

2. Which statement best summarizes the primary theme of the passage?

Answer: B - The passage emphasizes the dual nature of the revolution's legacy, showing both its positive contributions and its capacity for violence.

A can be ruled out because the revolution was not entirely negative- it brought about democracy in France. C is ruled out because it's too specific- the passage isn't only about the guillotine. D is ruled out because it is very absolute- ALL executions were not justified.

Detail Questions

It's unlikely you'll remember the exact answers for these, more the general location of the info. If it's asking for a number, I like to skim and look for numbers because they stand out in the text.

3. According to the passage, when did the Reign of Terror occur?

Answer: B - The passage explicitly states that the Reign of Terror lasted from September 1793 to July 1794.

These kind of detail questions, especially asking about numbers, just require you to go back and find the answer. If you can remember loosely where it was stated, start by looking there. When I answer questions about dates or numbers or percentages, I'm skimming looking for numbers to save time. The numbers stand out from the text.

4. Who developed the guillotine, according to the passage?

Answer: B - The passage clearly states that the guillotine was developed by Dr. Joseph-Ignace Guillotin in 1792.

This is one of those detail questions that you would have to make an approximate note of where the answer is. If you can remember it- great. What I'll usually do is remember that it said it somewhere in the first half, and start skimming for the answer. Try not to skim so fast that you overlook it, and when you find it double check that it's correct.

Inference Questions

These are the ones that require you to draw conclusions based on the information in the passage. Typically you should be able to rule out some of the oddball answers, and go with what seems most likely after that.

5. Based on the passage, what can be inferred about Dr. Guillotin's intentions when he proposed the guillotine?

Answer: C - The passage states that Dr. Guillotin proposed the device "as a more humane method of execution" compared to previous brutal methods.

Once again, this might require going back to find the answer and skimming for it. Some of the answers can also be eliminated with relative certainty- for example A and D seem a little odd.

6. What can be inferred about the public's reaction to guillotine executions during the Reign of Terror?

Answer: C - The passage states that executions "drew large crowds," indicating public attendance at these events. Everything else indicates that the guillotine executions were not public.

Vocabulary Questions

These require you to determine the meaning of a word based on the context of the passage. If I know what the word means I go with that. I think this is the exception to the rule of "not trusting prior information."" If I am not familiar with the word, Iskim, look at the context, try to rule out some of the oddball answers, and then make my best guess.

7. In the context of the passage, what does the word "tumultuous" most likely mean?

Answer: B - The context describes a period marked by revolution, violence, and dramatic changes, indicating disorder and confusion.

Tumultous is right at the beginning: "The French Revolution, which began in 1789, marked one of the most tumultuous periods in European history." There aren't a lot of clues as to what the word means there, so the best thing to do is make an informed decision based on the rest of the passage's tone. A and D can definitely be ruled out, and between B and C, I think B is the better choice.

8. Based on the passage, what does "egalitarian" most likely mean?

Answer: B - The passage describes the guillotine as a symbol of "egalitarian justice," and the context of the revolution emphasizes equality, suggesting "egalitarian" means believing in equality for all.

For this one I would think back to the guillotine inventor's intentions- a more humane method of execution. Since that doesn't give me a whole lot to go on, I think about the guillotine being intended as a symbol of the revolution and a justice for the common people- which leads me to make a best guess that the answer has to do with equality and justice.

Author's Purpose Questions

These type questions are really similar to main idea type questions. Typically you should be able to rule out some of the oddball answers, and go with what seems most likely after that.

9. What is the author's primary purpose in writing this passage?

Answer: B - The passage presents both the positive aspects (democratic ideals) and negative aspects (violence, terror) of the revolution, indicating an informative, balanced purpose.

A can be ruled out because the passage is not about celebrating the technological achievement. C can be ruled out because the passage is not about arguing that executions were necessary. D can be ruled out because the passage is not about promoting tourism to France. Between B and D, I think B is the better choice.

10. Why does the author include details about the guillotine's efficiency and the number of executions?

Answer: B - The author includes these details to illustrate the magnitude of the violence during the Reign of Terror, supporting the passage's theme about the dark side of the revolution.

Like the previous question, I would go by process of elimination and rule out the answers that don't make sense.
